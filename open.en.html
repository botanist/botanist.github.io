An open platform for everyone

We love open source and frankly without it we couldn't have made botani.st. For us it's natural that we too let others embrace and extend our platform the way they like - from APIs to protocols to electronics.

Publicly available data

The data collected by your and others sensors end up at our servers where it's analysed and stored. We can use this data for improving our plant database, do better predictions and all kinds of other imaginable stuff.

The "other imaginable stuff" we hope others have ideas for so by default all collected data will be publicly available . This includes not only the values but also some metadata such as positioning.

Infrastructural data and rules that controls your setup are however not public so you won't have to worry about someone hijacking your installation.

An open API

Our service that both our app and web client communicates with has a simple RESTful API. As data format JSON is used. Simple and elegant. And since it's very much all standard technologies your language of choice probably already has all that is needed.

Freedom of service

There might be situations where you don't want to use our service and the tools we provide but love to use our hardware both sensors and infrastructural.

At the core of each installation is one or more gateways that normally communicate with us. However they'll be checking first if there's a local service that will handle them or if the user has configured them to use another service. Very last they'll connect to us.

Gateways communicate with our service over a persistent TLS socket over a binary protocol. We'll be opening this protocol, which at the moment happens to be a GOB stream, and provide a stub implementation that you can build your own service around.

Firmware updates for our hardware will be available for download from us so your own service can also push out updates.

Build your own hardware

From the very start it's been important for us that it should be easy to build new sensors. We are using the standard I2C (also called TWI) serial computer bus for communicating between our hubs and wired sensors. Our hubs provide over-current, short-circuit and other means of protection for devices as they are meant to be used in humid or wet environments.

As well as providing full documentation on how to communicate with devices over I2C we'll be also providing an SDK for Atmel AVR microcontrollers and example schematics.

Support code, such as converting raw values to for example temperature, for a device type can be run on your gateway and is written in JavaScript.
